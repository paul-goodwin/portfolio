BuyingTime

A collaborative retail platform

Over seven months at Carnegie Mellon University (CMU, MHCI program) I was part of a team working with a client—LH Ventures (LHV)—to reimagine the business interactions between retail buyers and their wholesale suppliers. We prototyped a service oriented platform that makes retail buying visual, consolidated, and collaborative. I joined LHV to continue to develop our concepts into a successful startup currently launching with a billion dollar retailer client.


Challenge #1

Reimagine entrenched B2B relationships in 7 months

Every day retail buyers plan, source, and purchase of a range of products on behalf of a retailer that determines what is available to you, the consumer. The cumulative decisions of a single retail buyer can account for millions of dollars in purchases per year and directly affects the success of their company.


However, before the products ever make it onto the shelves, buyers encounter a dizzying array of emails, attachments, phone calls, and meetings to collaborate with wholesale organizations. This process was slow, inefficient, and frustrating for buyers across the board. Our challenge was to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of the retail buyer’s interaction with their wholesale supplier counterparts.


Laying the ground work

Business-to-business tools are too often created by one business and imposed on another. To paraphrase Conway's Law: organizations tend to design software that specific to their own needs and communication structures. Our initial stakeholder interviews showed both retail and supplier organizations had many complex and feature rich systems customized for their needs. However, the vital sales interactions between buyer and seller happens across companies and falls outside the domain of either companies' advanced systems.


How might we contribute to a buying expereince that crosses the boundries between companies and brings buyers and suppliers together seamlessly? Equally, how can we respect the long standing personal relationships and competative nature of the business? Why do buyers work with suppliers at all? We had to cast a wide net to start unpacking the implications of these early research questions.


Uncovering the Problems

I created our research field guide as a system of research activities to understand and connect a wide range of stakeholders’ needs and values. From interviews about why divisional merchandising manager care about sell-through rates, to a contextual inquiry into how products are negotiated during high profile New York meetings, to analysing what an assistant buyer does every monday before lunch. We captured and synthesised a wide range of activities that make up the buyer's role. For more details about the retail buying process check out our full research report.

Initial Synthesis

We captured the wove the details gathered from our research together using a modified affinity diagramming technique that used colour to highlight user roles. These colours allowed us to see clusters of user needs begin to form not just with our buyers, but throughout all of the connected retail roles. We used this information to construct flow diagrams, cultural models, personas, and as supporting evidence for the business and user value delivered by our prototype.

Deeper Insights

Buyers need actionable information to make the best decisions, but they are given hand-me-down financial, sales, and product spreadsheets adapted from other department’s designs. Although a retail buyer's process is much more complex than traditional ecommerce, the expereince doesn't have to be: it can be visual, it can be collaborative, and it can be fun.

Buyers can’t find the needle in the haystack. Buyers live and breathe products. However, to get a product’s full story, they have to cross-reference a muddled stack of sales spreadsheets, offers and meeting notes from vendors, and many other documents and emails. When a buyer transitions roles, getting up to speed on all this product information can take months (especially when half the information is lost to the previous buyer’s email account).


Buying Time centralizes, visualizes, and keeps a record of all products, allowing buyers to find the product level information they need

Buyers struggle to put the puzzle together. Buyers engage in many different "qualitative" activities: gathering insights from comp shopping, observing their customers in-store, and discussing trends with vendors. These activities, though critical to strategy, are typically very adhoc and unstructured. As it stands, most buyers work from a general gist of this information, rather than specifics, because it is too cumbersome to organize.


Buying Time helps organize market strategy into buying programs and easily communicate them with vendors.

Purchase Orders are 20% work, and 80% headache. Communication challenges plague the current purchase order (PO) process. Issues between buyers and vendors can cause late shipments, missed opportunities, and strained relationships. This process is critical to retail buying, and improving it is our core focus—maximizing both buyer and retailer benefit, as well as value to LH Ventures


Buying Time’s service approach helps buyers feel decisive and empowered: they can now create a purchase order with one click. No more headaches.

What Happened to the Thrill? Buying creates a unique high that is part shopping, part strategic gambling, and part competition. But now it’s followed by a low: the paperwork. A successful tool needs to lessen the lows without sacrificing the highs. It also needs to work both for in-person meetings and remote purchasing.


Buying Time is flexible. It works seamlessly when the buyer and vendor are meeting in-person or discussing products remotely
